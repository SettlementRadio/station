## The speed of ships and the weight of distance

There is no faster-than-light. There is only the long burn, the coast, the long deceleration. A ship pushes against the void with engines that have changed little in centuries — more efficient, perhaps, more reliable, but still bound by the same equations. The crossings between neighbouring worlds take weeks because that is how long it takes to move that far at the speeds physics permits.

Ships carry the heavy things: ore, grain, machinery, the bodies of people who have time to spend weeks in transit. They carry the things that cannot be transmitted by radio, the things that must arrive as matter rather than signal. A ship is a small world, self-contained, recycling air and water and patience. The crews develop their own rhythms, their own temporary societies, their own ways of marking time across the void.

The slowness is not an inconvenience to be solved. It is a fact of the universe, accepted the way one accepts weather or gravity. People plan around it. They do not expect quick passage. They do not complain about the weeks because there is no alternative to complain toward. The slowness shapes everything: commerce, politics, love, grief. A death in the family arrives by radio long before the body can follow. A birth announced by signal is celebrated twice: once when the news arrives, once when the infant finally makes the crossing.

## Energy and the economics of scarcity

Fusion is old technology, reliable, the workhorse of settled space. Every world has its reactors, every ship its engine, every habitat its backup. The fuel is hydrogen, abundant, scattered across the dark in gas giants and ice and the thin interstellar medium. What is scarce is not the energy itself but the machines that turn it into useful work.

A reactor is a delicate thing, full of magnetic bottles and controlled plasma and parts that must be replaced on schedules measured in decades. The factories that build them are few, and the knowledge of how to build them is concentrated in the hands of people who learned from people who learned from the first generation. A broken reactor on a frontier world might wait months for a part, years for a technician skilled enough to install it. This is why the worlds prize reliability over innovation. A machine that works is better than a machine that works better but might break.

Solar is ubiquitous on worlds with stars, cheap and low-maintenance, but weak out at the frontier worlds, far from any sun. Batteries are heavy, expensive, prone to degradation. Wind and hydro and geothermal fill niches where the geology permits. But fusion is the baseline, the thing everyone expects, the technology that makes the settled worlds possible.

## The machines of daily life

Automation is everywhere and invisible. The farms run themselves, monitored by people who intervene only when the unexpected happens. The water recyclers hum in the walls, cleaning and reclaiming, requiring only the occasional filter change. The factories that matter — the ones that make air processors and medical equipment and the parts that keep other factories running — are tended by machines that tend themselves.

But the frontier is different. Out there, a broken machine might stay broken until someone improvises a fix from whatever is at hand. People learn to repair, to adapt, to coax function from devices that were never meant to last so long. There is a pride in it, a culture of jury-rigging and make-do. Those in the dense core look down on this as backward, but the frontier sees it as survival, as the skill that keeps a settlement alive when the next ship is eight weeks away.

The things people use directly are simple, durable, repairable. A radio that can be fixed with a soldering iron. A water pump with parts that can be machined from scrap. The worlds learned early that complexity is a luxury of proximity, that a thing you cannot fix yourself is a liability when help is measured in weeks of transit.

## Medicine and the body

Human bodies have not changed. Lifespan is still measured in decades, illness still happens, death still comes. Medicine can do much — mend bones, fight infection, manage the chronic conditions — but it cannot do everything. The serious things, the things that require specialised equipment or rare drugs, require facilities that exist only in the dense core or on the largest stations.

A sick person on a frontier world faces a choice: hope the local medic can manage it, or commit to weeks of transit to reach better care. Some conditions cannot wait. Some people choose to die at home rather than spend their last days in a ship's cramped quarters, chasing help that might arrive too late.

There are no miracles. No regeneration of limbs, no reversal of ageing, no copying of a mind out of its body. The body is the body, fragile and stubborn and limited, and medicine works within those limits. People accept this, mostly. They have to. The alternative is to wish for technologies that do not exist and probably never will.

## The hard ceiling

The limits are not temporary. They are not problems awaiting a solution. They are baked into the physics of the universe, accepted by engineers and planners and ordinary people as facts as immovable as gravity. No one is researching faster-than-light. No one is promising a breakthrough that will make the crossings instant. The universe has spoken, and the answer is no.

This is why the radio matters. This is why a voice carried by signal, delayed but immediate in a way that ships can never be, is the thread that binds the worlds. Technology has given the settlers fusion and automation and medicine enough to survive, but it has not given them escape from distance. They live within the limits, building their lives around the hard facts of time and space, finding meaning in the connections that survive despite the lag.

The wonder is not in what technology makes possible. It is in what people do with the possible, how they build lives and societies and meaning within the constraints. The rigour is the point. The limits are the shape of the world.
