## The local nature of distant justice

With courts and help weeks away, justice is mostly local and immediate, resting on shared compacts every settled person is taught to honour. A crime committed on a frontier station cannot wait for a judge to arrive from the core; the evidence grows cold, the witnesses scatter, the community's need for resolution becomes urgent. So justice devolves to those present: the station administrator, the council of elders, the assembled crew, the captain of the ship. They apply not statutory law but custom — the accumulated wisdom of what has worked before, what has kept the peace, what has let communities survive in conditions where survival is never guaranteed.

This local justice is not arbitrary. The Settlement Compacts provide a framework, a set of principles agreed across the worlds, taught to every child, invoked in every dispute. The Compacts are not a code; they are a tradition, a way of thinking about justice that puts restoration over punishment, community over individual, survival over principle. A Compact judge asks not "what law was broken?" but "what harm was done, and how can it be mended?" — not "who is guilty?" but "how do the parties go on living together?"

The Compacts vary in their specifics. Some worlds emphasise restitution: the thief must repay, the vandal must repair, the debt must be settled in kind. Others emphasise reconciliation: the parties must speak, must understand each other, must find a way to resume their relationship. Others emphasise exclusion: those who cannot live by the community's rules must leave, must find other communities or make their way alone. The choice among these depends on the nature of the harm, the history of the parties, the resources of the community, the judgement of those empowered to decide.

## Rights and their limits

Certain rights are held to be owed to every settled person regardless of world; their limits are argued, but the core is common ground. The Right to Breathe is the most fundamental: no one may be denied the air, water, and food necessary for survival. This right trumps property, trumps punishment, trumps the community's convenience. A station that casts someone into the void without suit and supply commits not merely a crime but a sin against the fundamental condition of settlement. The right is not generous; it is pragmatic. The settled worlds are too fragile, too interdependent, to let anyone be treated as disposable.

The Right to Work follows from the right to breathe. Every person able to contribute must be allowed to contribute; every person who contributes must be sustained by the community's labour. This is not charity but exchange: the community provides survival, the individual provides effort. Those who refuse to work may be denied the comforts beyond survival, assigned the least desirable tasks, treated with the contempt the settled worlds reserve for freeloaders. But they cannot be starved, cannot be expelled, cannot be abandoned to the void.

The Right to Speak is more contested. The Compacts guarantee the freedom to voice grievance, to call for redress, to argue for change. But this freedom is balanced against the community's need for order, for the ability to make decisions and enforce them. A speaker who incites violence, who undermines the community's capacity to survive, who turns words into weapons against the vulnerable, may be silenced, excluded, denied the platform the community provides. The line is drawn differently on different worlds, argued endlessly, adjusted to circumstance.

The Right to Leave is the final guarantee. No one may be held against their will on a world or station where they do not wish to remain. This right is limited by practicality: the one who wishes to leave must find a ship willing to carry them, must have the means to sustain themselves on arrival, must accept that departure may mean never returning. But the right itself is absolute. A community that prevents departure becomes a prison, and prisons are remembered in the history of the settled worlds as failures — places where the Compacts were broken and the consequences were terrible.

## Crime and redress

Crime in the settled worlds is defined not by statute but by harm: acts that damage the community's capacity to survive, that violate the rights of others, that undermine the trust cooperation depends on. Theft is grave not because property is sacred but because resources are scarce, because to take what another needs is to threaten their survival. Violence is grave because the means to treat injury are limited, because a person incapacitated is a person who cannot contribute, because the habit of violence spreads and threatens the peace on which all depend. Deception is grave because the settled worlds run on information — on the trust that what is reported is true, that what is promised will be delivered, that a given word can be relied upon.

The response to crime follows the logic of restoration. The thief must return what was taken, or its equivalent, or work to replace it. The violent must make their victim whole, must provide the labour the victim cannot, must show that they have learned control. The deceitful must own their lies, must repair the damage of false information, must accept the diminished trust that will follow them. Punishment as such is rare, kept for those who cannot or will not accept restoration, who repeat their harms, who show that they cannot live by the Compacts.

Exile is the final sanction. Those who cannot be restored, who threaten the community's survival by their presence, who have exhausted the patience of their fellows, may be sent away. Exile is not death; the exile is given supplies, equipment, the means to seek another community or to survive alone if they can. But exile is grave. The exile leaves behind everyone they know, everything familiar, the protection of the community they have forfeited. They enter the between — the dangerous space of ships and stations where they are unknown, untrusted, unwelcome until they prove themselves again. Exile into the dark is among the gravest sentences, kept for the most serious violations, imposed with sorrow rather than anger.

## The law of ships and the between

The ships and the between answer to no single world, so the law of transit is old custom and mutual need. A ship is a community unto itself, bound by the captain's authority, the crew's obligations, the passengers' temporary submission. The captain's word is law during transit, subject only to the ultimate authority of the ship's owners and the customs that govern the treatment of crew and passengers. A captain who abuses this authority may find themselves unable to hire crew, unable to secure cargo, unable to dock at stations where their reputation has spread. The market enforces what no court can reach.

The Law of Salvage governs the between: the right to claim abandoned vessels, the obligation to rescue those in distress, the sharing of reward among those who risk their own safety to help others. Salvage is honourable work, respected across the worlds, governed by customs that make sure assistance is given and that those who give it are repaid. A ship that ignores a distress signal is shunned; a ship that claims salvage dishonestly is pursued. The customs are enforced by reputation, by the network of captains and crews who share information, by the plain necessity that those who travel the between must be able to trust each other.

Piracy lives in the gap between worlds, where no authority is present and enforcement is distant. The response to piracy is not legal but practical: armed ships, convoys, the refusal of ports to trade with known raiders. A captured pirate may be tried by the nearest authority, may be exiled or punished according to local custom, may be ransomed or exchanged. There is no consistent law, only the accumulation of practices that have proven effective at suppression. The worlds have not agreed whether piracy is a crime against all humanity or a local offence, whether captured raiders are prisoners of war or common criminals, whether death is a just sentence or an excessive one. Each case is decided locally, by the customs of the capturing authority, the severity of the offence, the politics of the moment.

## The Settlement Council and the dream of common law

The Settlement Council represents the aspiration toward common law — toward a justice that transcends the local and applies equally across the worlds. The Council has no enforcement power; its authority rests on the willingness of worlds to submit disputes to its judgement, to accept its recommendations, to treat its pronouncements as binding. This willingness varies. The core, densely interconnected, appeals to the Council often; the frontier, distant and independent, rarely does. The Council's docket fills with trade disputes, with questions of jurisdiction, with appeals from parties who believe local justice has failed them.

The Council's procedures are slow, deliberate, shaped by the distance that separates its members. A case may take years: the submission of documents, the appointment of mediators, the hearing of testimony, the deliberation of judges who may never meet in person, who confer through the relays, who render judgements that arrive weeks after they are composed. The Council is not a court of first resort but of last — the place parties go when local justice has failed, when the stakes are high enough to justify the delay, when the principle matters more than the practical outcome.

The Council's greatest achievement is the Common Trade Code, a set of rules governing trade between worlds that has been adopted, with local variation, across the settled worlds. The Code provides certainty: contracts made in one jurisdiction will be honoured in another, debts incurred will be repaid, fraud will be answered. The Code is enforced not by the Council but by the market: those who break it find themselves unable to secure credit, unable to find partners, unable to take part in the trade that sustains the worlds. It is law without enforcement, custom with the force of necessity behind it.

The Council's greatest limitation is its inability to reach the individual — to give justice to the person wronged by their own community, to protect the rights the Compacts guarantee. A person on a frontier station who believes their rights have been violated has no appeal to the Council; the Council has no presence there, no power to enforce its judgements, no way to reach the individual without the cooperation of the very authority that has wronged them. The Council is a forum for worlds, not for people; its justice is abstract, distant, slow. The person who needs justice now must seek it locally, must accept the judgement of those present, must hope the Compacts are honoured in their case.
