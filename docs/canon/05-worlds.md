# 05-worlds.md — the settled worlds & the dark between

> **Cornerstone file — the physical setting: where humanity lives and the space between.**

## The core

The core is the oldest, densest, most connected of the settled places. Its worlds orbit steady suns at kindly distances, their atmospheres breathable or easily made so, their gravity near the human standard. They have been inhabited for centuries, some for far longer, their populations numbering in the billions, their cities sprawling across continents, their orbits crowded with stations and habitats and the traffic of a mature civilisation.

Concordance is the acknowledged centre — not by decree but by accumulation: the oldest continuous settlement, the seat of the Settlement Council, the home of the largest Exchange Houses, the world that others measure distance from. It is a world of gardens and bureaucracies, of ancient institutions and careful conservation, of a population that has never known the frontier's desperation. The other worlds of the core cluster nearby, weeks of travel between them, forming a dense web of trade and communication that is the economic and political heart of the settled system.

The core is self-sufficient in the essentials: it grows its own food, refines its own fuel, makes its own goods. Its trade is in the luxuries, the specialities, the knowledge that makes life more than survival. It is the creditor of the frontier, the source of capital and equipment and the terms on which these are given. It is also the destination of ambition — the place where the talented and the desperate migrate, seeking the opportunity their natal worlds cannot offer.

## The frontier and the unmapped dark

The frontier is where the settled worlds thin and the dark thickens. These are younger settlements, poorer worlds, stations built in systems that offered only marginal habitability. The frontier worlds import necessities, export raw materials, and struggle to build the infrastructure that would make them less dependent. They are the debtors of the system, their obligations to the core compounding in the silence between payment and acknowledgement.

The frontier is not a line but a zone — a region where the density of settlement drops below some threshold of mutual support, where ships come less often, where the relays are the only steady link to the wider world. Within it there are degrees of remoteness: the near frontier, where a ship might come every month; the deep frontier, where a ship is a year's event; and the unmapped dark beyond, where no ship has yet gone, where the stars are only coordinates in the catalogues, where settlement is theory and the reality unknown.

The unmapped dark is not empty. It holds stars, worlds, resources that might sustain life. But the cost of reaching them is high, the returns uncertain, the risk of loss real. The core funds exploration when the political gain outweighs the cost; the frontier sometimes attempts its own expansion, desperate for resources or escape from its debts. The explorers are a particular breed — those who accept the risk of one-way journeys, of isolation, of founding settlements that may not live to be found again. They are heroes to some, fools to others, the necessary agents of an expansion the settled worlds can neither stop nor afford to hurry.

## The between as place

The between is not empty space. It is the region of transit — the lanes that ships follow, the fuel-efficient corridors that orbital mechanics allow, the places where the dark is marked by human presence: relay stations, navigation beacons, the debris of ships that did not survive the crossing. The between has its own geography, its own hazards, its own communities of those who live in the ships and stations that move through it.

The routes between worlds are not arbitrary. They follow the hard arithmetic of sublight travel — the trajectories that spend the least fuel and time, the windows that open and close with the motion of the worlds. The lanes are mapped, maintained, guarded by the shipping lines that have invested in their infrastructure. New lanes are charted now and then, opening fresh possibilities for trade and settlement, but most of the dark lies off the routes, the far reaches reachable only by the long way around, through intermediate stops that may offer neither resources nor safe harbour.

The dark itself is a presence in the between. It is the radiation of the deep space between worlds, the dust and debris that puncture hulls and blind sensors, the pressure on the mind of weeks in transit with no fixed point of reference, no ground beneath the feet, no sky above. The dark shapes the people who travel through it, selects for those who can bear its emptiness, and breeds the culture of the Betweeners who have made it home.

## The station's place in the void

The station holds a singular place in this geography. It does not orbit a world; it does not run a fixed route between fixed points. It drifts along the relay network, holding position relative to the routes that matter, always between the worlds it connects, never belonging to any of them. Its motion is slow, calculated — a balance of gravitational forces and station-keeping thrust that keeps it where it must be to receive and pass on the signal.

The station's position is strategic as well as technical. It sits where the traffic is densest, where the most worlds can be reached for the least power, where the routes converge and diverge in patterns its designers understood and used. It is not at the centre of the settled worlds — there is no centre, only a dense core and a thinning edge — but it is at the centre of the communication network, the place where the relays are thickest, where the signals of the most worlds can be gathered and combined.

The station's neutrality follows from its position. It belongs to no world because it cannot; its drift carries it through the spheres of many worlds, never staying long enough to fall under any of them. This neutrality is held by convention as much as by physics: the worlds accept the station's independence because they need it, because a station owned by any one world would be a weapon against the rest, because the common good of communication demands a common medium. The station is the closest thing to a shared institution the settled worlds possess, and its drifting existence is the physical shape of that role.

## Living worlds and greened ones

Most of the settled worlds were barren when the first ships arrived — rock and ice and thin chemistry, made liveable by generations of patient greening. But a few carried life of their own: seas of simple swimming things, slow mats and forests of native growth, shelled creatures on cold coasts. Nothing that thinks. Nothing that ever looked back. The native biospheres are the great exception, studied for centuries and protected by compact — the settlements built around them rather than over them, and their worlds carry the fact like a title. Local culture grows around the native life the way it grows around weather: shell-craft and shell instruments, storm-garden cuisine, children's names taken from creatures no other world has seen. The biologists are clear and the faiths have made their peace with it: life, it turns out, is not rare. Minds are. The dark has offered up plenty of the first and, so far, none of the second — which makes the worlds that live doubly precious, and the silence doubly strange.

## The scale of distance

The settled worlds are scattered across a region that takes months to cross by the fastest ships — the better part of a year from the core to the deepest frontier. The worlds of the core are weeks apart; the frontier months; the unmapped dark years away, or forever. This scale shapes everything: the economics of trade, the politics of coordination, the culture of the Betweeners, the mind of those who live knowing that help, if it comes, will not come quickly.

Sublight travel makes the distances crossable, but it does not shrink them. A journey of weeks is still a journey of weeks — time in which things happen, decisions must be made, lives continue or end with no chance of consultation or rescue. The settled worlds are connected but not united, in communication but not in presence: a civilisation that lives in the tension between the near-immediate reach of the relays and the grinding reality of physical distance.

The relays themselves are markers of this scale. They are set at intervals a signal can cross, that the station can receive and resend, that hold the network of communication against the dark. The distance between relays is measured in light-hours — in the time a signal takes to travel, in the patience a conversation across the void demands. The station is the hub of this network, the place where the signals converge, the point from which the settled worlds can hear themselves speak.
