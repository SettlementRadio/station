## Workwear first

All settled dress descends from workwear. Clothes out here are equipment before they are anything else — thermal layers for the cold worlds, storm gear for Meridian's coasts, the close-fitting shipsuit whose every pocket has a reason, the dock jacket built to take a tool's weight and a cable's snag. A world's look is readable from its work and its weather the way its accent is readable from its distance: you can place a stranger to within a few worlds by their boots. And because the frontier ethos holds the display of excess to be vulgar, distinction went where it could: into detail. The cut of a collar, the closures, the stitching, whose hands did the mending and how visibly — settled dress is a quiet language of exactly such small things, fluent everywhere, showy nowhere.

Mending is the heart of it. Cloth crosses the dark at freight prices, so a garment is kept in service for decades, and the mending is done openly — contrast thread, deliberate patches, the repair displayed rather than hidden. A patched jacket carries its history on its surface: this tear from the dock, that burn from the engine room, the shoulder rebuilt by a partner's hands. "Worn well" is a real compliment, and it is aimed at the wearer as much as the coat.

## Cloth and where it comes from

Every world's cloth names its origin. Fibre comes from what a world can grow or culture — vat-silk on the stations, coarse crop-fibres on the agricultural worlds, real wool and cotton from the garden worlds of the core, moving in small lots at luxury prices with the fruit. Dyes come from local chemistry, so a world's palette is very nearly its geology: Cold Harbor's greys and sea-glass blues, Forge's rust and char, the unlikely greens the garden worlds can afford. A trained eye reads a bolt of cloth like a manifest — what it is made of says where it is from, and what it cost says how far it has come.

That is why a textile is the classic heirloom of the settled worlds. A length of home-world cloth carried across generations — re-cut, re-sewn, never discarded — outlasts the memory of the world it came from and stands in for it. Handling a family's textile is handling their map.

## The asynchronous season

Fashion crosses the dark at two speeds, like everything else. A cut, a pattern, a way of wearing a collar — those are information, and they ride the broadcast tier: a style can sweep the settled worlds in weeks as pure description. But the cloth itself, the actual made garment, travels by ship. So every fashion arrives everywhere twice — first as news, then as freight — months apart, and by the time the garments arrive the local tailors have already built their own version from the description, better suited to local cloth and local weather. The style comes back changed, and both versions keep the name. Who wore it first is unanswerable across the lag, and argued anyway, with pleasure.

Formality is owned by occasions, not wealth. Nearly everyone keeps one good garment — the arrival coat, most worlds call it — worn for arrivals and departures, for weddings, and for the Sending of the Dead: the moments when the settled worlds dress up are exactly the moments someone crosses a threshold. Festival dress is light-work: Lumen garments carry lamplight thread and polished closures, made to be seen by lamplight in the year's warmest dark.
