## The two-room clinic

On most settled worlds, medicine is a clinic of two rooms and a medic who is also something else — hydroponicist, teacher, water engineer — because no skill travels alone out there. The clinic holds what a clinic can hold: the diagnostic bench, the drug locker, the drilled competence of one or two people, and the casebooks. Serious medicine — the surgeries that need a theatre, the drugs that need a fabricator, the conditions that need a specialist's hands — lives only in the dense core and on the largest stations, weeks of transit away. So the frontier's first medical art is triage of a particular kind: not *who first*, but *here or away* — what can be managed in these two rooms, and what begins the old, hard conversation about a ship.

Because the nearest specialist answers in weeks, frontier medicine is protocol medicine. The medic's shelf of checklists and drilled procedures is not a poor cousin of core practice; it is its own discipline, honed exactly for the case where the expert cannot be reached and the second chance does not come. Every settled adult carries the basic competences — air, wounds, resuscitation — as part of the common schooling, and a settlement drills its medical emergencies the way it drills its pressure drills: bored, regular, and grateful for the boredom.

## The correspondence of physicians

The profession holds itself together on the queue. A medic with a hard case writes it up — history, readings, what was tried — and sends it to the physician-correspondence network: the standing exchange of consult letters that rides the relays between every clinic and academy in the settled worlds. Answers come back in weeks, from doctors under other skies who have seen something like it, disagree with each other, and say so. The exchange is archived, appended, and argued into the relay casebooks — the profession's slow shared memory — so every hard case treated anywhere eventually teaches everywhere. A famous unresolved case runs for years and is followed like a serial; medics name their favourites.

The cruelty and the dignity of the network is its timing. A consult's answer routinely arrives for a patient who is already recovered or already buried. Physicians answer anyway, carefully, at length — the reply was never only for this patient; it is for the casebook, and the next one. *Write for the next case* is the first thing the correspondence teaches, and the closest thing the profession has to a creed.

Some physicians still ride instead of write. The circuit physicians travel scheduled loops of small settlements the way the Circuit Scholars do — a year or more to a loop, carrying medicine, technique, fresh casebook pages, and news of who lives. The tradition descends from Verene Mere's Silence-era crossings, and the old phrase still holds: to serve a stretch of cut-off worlds well is *riding the Mercy Round*, and there is no higher compliment in the trade.

## Quarantine and the sealed world

In a closed habitat, air is held in common, and so is anything that rides it. Illness in a station is an engineering event as much as a medical one: sickness moves along the ducts and corridors the engineers know by heart, and an outbreak response looks like maintenance — sectioning, sealed doors, the clean corridor, the numbers watched shift by shift. The oldest habit of settled public health is architectural, and every station drill assumes it.

Ships hold quarantine at dock. The length is the port's custom — days, usually, set by the harbourmaster and the medic together, adjusted for where the ship has been — and crews grumble through it in dockside quarters built for exactly that grumbling. Nobody seriously argues. A sealed dock is not unfriendliness; it is carefulness, and the settled worlds remember why. Ports signal a closed dock with a steady amber lamp, and "riding out the amber" is every spacer's least favourite fluency.

## The pharmacopoeia and the cold chain

Drugs divide the way everything divides out here: what a world can make, and what must cross the dark. The basics — the analgesics, the common antibiotics, the field synthesises — most settlements produce locally, and the *formulas* travel at broadcast speed, checked and countersigned across the network. But the complex drugs need a core fabricator, and those cross at ship speed, in the refrigerated holds of the Medical Transports, on schedules every clinic knows by heart. That difference — the formula in hours, the finished drug in months — is the health gap between core and frontier, stated as physics.

So the cold chain is watched like weather. A Medical Transport running late is front-page news on every world it serves; clinics lend stock across neighbouring settlements on standing trust, argue expiry margins with the seriousness the numbers deserve, and keep the shortage arithmetic — who is owed what, which locker holds the last of it — as carefully as any Exchange House keeps its ledgers. Asking after a neighbour world's drug locker is, on the frontier, a normal courtesy of correspondence.

## What illness means across the lag

Between worlds, illness travels as letters. The bundle that brings word that a father is sick may be the bundle that brings word he is well again — "by the time you read this I am recovered" is among the most-written sentences in the settled worlds, and its darker sibling is written carefully and read slowly. Families weigh passage against odds and against physics: weeks of transit means arriving to the middle of a story, or its end. Some go anyway; some write instead, and the long letters kept from an illness — read aloud at the Sending of the Dead, sometimes — are their own quiet literature.

At home, care is communal because everything is. The household nurses its sick the way it raises its children — in shifts, by rota, with the neighbours' claim on the work taken for granted — and a settlement's real health system is this: the clinic at the centre, and around it the mesh of people who notice a missed meal, a missed shift, a light left on too long. Nobody on a settled world is sick alone unless they have worked very hard at it.
