## Concordance

The acknowledged centre of the core — not by decree but by accumulation. Concordance is the oldest continuous settlement, the seat of the Settlement Council, the home of the largest Exchange Houses, and the world the others measure distance from. It is a world of gardens and bureaucracies, ancient institutions and careful conservation, a population that has never known the frontier's desperation. Standard speech is kept here; the synth-harpsichord is its signature instrument, a sound at once ancient and new. To the frontier, Concordance is the creditor, the standard-setter, the distant weather of politics; to its own people it is simply the place where things are decided slowly and kept long.

## Meridian

A core world with a famous temper. Meridian's storm coast is walled with lighthouses — working ones, tended by keepers (the station's own Vell held one before the microphone) — and its people speak of the Storm as a presence, the great atmospheric engine that shaped their world and their music. When the storm season seals the doors for a month of night, the coast dances indoors: pulse-dance was born here, joy manufactured exactly where the weather withholds it. In the calm seasons the atmospheric racing league threads fliers through the same storm systems. Meridian's twin moons align once every eleven years to swallow its sun for seven minutes — the famous eclipse, during which it is bad luck to speak.

## Cold Harbor

An ice-and-ocean world at the frontier's near edge, named plainly for what it is. Cold Harbor's year swings between a brief bright thaw — the frozen sea cracks, ships move between its settlements, reunions and mournings arrive together — and the long night, which its people personify as something that waits. They race on the ice through the dark months, engines singing on the frozen sea, and the racing is how the rest of the settled worlds know them: hard people, warm doors, a dry humour that other worlds mistake for gloom until they've been inside one winter.

## Forge

The industrial world. Forge builds the heavy things — hulls, reactor housings, the machinery other worlds repair for decades — and its sprawl of workshops gave the settled worlds a signature sound: the resonance pipes, long alloy tubes first built as signalling devices and later bowed and struck as instruments. Its workshops still turn industrial waste into resonators, and its news is watched everywhere because what Forge makes, everyone eventually depends on. It is a proud, sooty, musical place; the station's Thorn learned the news trade as a stringer here.

## Halcyon

A core world whose name promises more peace than its committee meetings deliver. Old, comfortable, garden-rich, and locally convinced it should have been the seat of the Council; the rest of the core finds this endearing and does not tell Halcyon so.

## Ashfall

A volcanic world that wears its name honestly. Generations ago an eruption buried its largest settlement — the station had been broadcasting to them moments before, and the annual Ashfall Minute still marks the hour the signal died. Ashfall rebuilt smaller, further from the mountains, and its people are known for a particular steadiness: they live on a world that has already shown them the worst, and they stay.

## Far Reach and Breathe Easy

Frontier stations whose names do the describing. Far Reach is exactly that — one of the last crewed nodes before the relay network thins into the dark zones, a place travellers pass through and remember. Breathe Easy began as a warning that went unheeded (its first air plant was famously unreliable) and became a joke that stuck, then a name no one would now change; its air, for the record, has been fine for generations.

## The Old System

Earth's home system — Earth itself, the red-soil greenhouses of Mars, the ice-roofed colonies of Europa, the orange harbours of Titan, the worked rocks of the Belt, the old Lagrange stations. It is inhabited, ordinary, and *not counted among the settled worlds*: the old system is what the settled worlds left, spoken of the way Earth is spoken of — origin, not neighbourhood — and it belongs to no core and no frontier. The journey there takes years, made rarely by the few who can afford it; the relay road to it is the longest and thinnest in the network, so word crosses in seasons, and its records and music arrive late and are treasured for it — a new old-system record is old news at home and brand new on the settled worlds' air, like a letter from a grandparent. What the songs describe — dock shifts under Saturn, terraforming votes, storm static on the Mars relay — is the settled worlds' own life in an older key, which is exactly why they love it.

## ES-447

Not a settlement — a catalogue entry. An empty world at the far edge of the settled dark, known to listeners only because Zhe records there: no people, no lights, only the listening equipment and the wind. It stands in for all the places the charts name and no one has made home.
