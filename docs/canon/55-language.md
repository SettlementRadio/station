## The common tongue and its limits

The settled worlds share a language, or something close to it. The Common Speech descended from the dominant tongues of the early settlement era, standardised out of practical necessity — crews needed to communicate, stations needed to coordinate, the machinery of survival demanded mutual intelligibility. But centuries have passed, and the lag of distance separates world from world. The Common Speech has not stayed common.

The core, densely interconnected, has preserved something close to the original — standardised, regulated, taught in schools. They call it Standard, and they enforce it through education and broadcast. The frontier has grown Frontier Common, a simplified version, stripped of grammatical complexity, heavy with loan-words from local conditions, from the particular dangers and resources of each world. A speaker of Standard and a speaker of Frontier Common can understand each other, but the effort shows — the pauses, the repetitions, the gestures that fill in for missing vocabulary.

The outer stations have grown something else entirely. Station Dialects emerge in isolation, shaped by the conditions of each habitat — the technical vocabulary of life support, the slang of long-haul crews, the shorthand of cramped communities. Some stations have accents so thick that other speakers struggle. A few have made grammatical innovations: new tenses for the long gap between a word sent and an answer received, new pronouns for the relationship between the first settlers and their descendants. These spread slowly, if at all — a station's dialect is its identity, and change comes only from the rare traveller, the occasional broadcast, the slow drift of influence.

## The drift of meaning

Words change meaning as they travel. A word spoken in the core arrives at a frontier station long after, and in the meantime it has gathered new associations, new implications, new weight. The frontier hears the word and understands something slightly different from what was meant. They answer, and their answer travels back, gathering its own drift. By the time the first speaker hears the reply, the conversation has become a game of telephone across the void.

Some words have drifted so far they now mean opposite things on different worlds. *Settlement* itself is the classic case: in the core it means a permanent community, a place of stability and law; on some frontier stations it means a temporary arrangement, a camp to be struck when the resources run out. A politician in the core speaks of "strengthening the settlements" and a frontier listener hears a promise to prolong their temporary misery. A frontier speaker describes their "settlement" and a listener in the core imagines infrastructure, institutions, a permanence that does not exist.

Other fossil-words persist in forms that betray their origins. *Ship* is still used for any vessel that crosses between worlds, though most have never touched water. *Crew* applies to any group that works together, though the nautical sense is long gone. *Station* once meant a fixed point, a place that does not move, and now names habitats that orbit, that rotate, that are never still relative to anything. The words survive because they are useful, but they carry their history with them, ghosts of meanings that no longer apply.

## Naming the worlds and their people

The names of places reveal their histories. The worlds of the core bear names that reach for aspiration and origin: Concordance, Meridian, Halcyon. The frontier's worlds and stations more often bear names that describe their conditions or their work: the ice world Cold Harbor, the stations Far Reach and Breathe Easy. Some names are jokes that stuck, or warnings that went unheeded, or memorials to the dead. A station's name is rarely changed; the bureaucratic effort is too great, and the name has become part of the identity of the people who live under it.

Personal names follow patterns that reveal family origins and cultural affiliations. Core names tend toward the compound and descriptive: Anwen Sael-Okorie, Tomas Vidal-Renn. Frontier names are often shorter — sometimes single names taken by choice, sometimes a description of a function or a trait: Two, Sister Wire, Patch. Some frontier communities have naming conventions that mark generation — First, Second, Third — counting how long a family has been in the void. The Children of the Signal take names that reference transmission and frequency: Carrier, Bandwidth, Static.

Ship names are a genre to themselves. They range from the utilitarian (Hauler 7, Supply Module 4) to the poetic (The Far Shore, Between Stars, Last Light). Some ships are named for their destinations, some for their origins, some for hopes or fears. The tradition of naming ships for women persists in some communities, though the reason is largely forgotten. The tradition of naming ships for abstract virtues — Hope, Endurance, Persistence — is just as common and just as mysterious in origin.

## Loan-words and untranslatables

Each world grows vocabulary for its own conditions. The words spread slowly, carried by travellers and broadcasts, settling into the Common Speech like sediment. From the high-gravity worlds come words for the particular exhaustion of heavy living: *drag*, *weight*, *ground-sick*. From the stations come words for the psychology of enclosed spaces: *cabin*, *bulkhead*, *recycle* as a verb that means to process and reuse not only materials but experiences, stories, relationships. From the frontier come words for the dangers of survival: *blowout*, *scrubber*, *ration* as a state of being rather than a measured quantity.

Some concepts resist translation. The traditions of impermanence carry a word for the passage between one life and the next; it has stretched to cover the long crossings, the liminal existence of travellers between worlds, and it holds an emotional weight no Standard equivalent captures. The old absorptive cosmologies carry a word for duty and right action and cosmic order; it has stretched to include the obligations of settlement — the duty to survive, to build, to carry culture across the dark. Both are spoken even in Standard, but with a sense of approximation, of reaching toward a meaning that does not quite fit.

The Children of the Signal have made technical vocabulary into a sacred language: *frequency*, *modulation*, *carrier wave* used with theological precision. The Betweeners have words for the psychology of perpetual motion: *arrival-sickness*, *the staying*, *ground-need*. These are understood by other speakers, but their full resonance belongs only to those who have lived what they name.

## The relays and the slowing of drift

The relays slow the drift of language, but they do not stop it. When Settlement Radio broadcasts, it speaks a version of Standard understood across the settled worlds. The DJs are trained in the common tongue, or they come from the core where Standard is kept. Their broadcasts give a reference point, a shared source of vocabulary and pronunciation that other speakers can steer toward.

But the effect is partial. The station broadcasts for hours each day, yet the worlds turn, the stations orbit, the signal is not always there. Listeners hear the station and bend their speech toward what they hear, but they also speak to each other, growing local patterns, local inventions. The station slows the drift; it does not reverse it. The languages of the settled worlds keep diverging, only more slowly than they would without the common reference.

The station also seeds new vocabulary. Words coined on one world, broadcast outward, heard on others, enter the common tongue — a genre name like Void Ballads or Frontier Reels, *the drift* as the name for the cultural time-lag between worlds. These began in particular communities, spread through the station's broadcasts, became shared. The station is a source of invention as much as of conservation.

The result is a language at once unified and various. Speakers across the settled worlds can understand each other — with effort, with patience, with the occasional confusion. But they also speak local variants, rich with their own vocabulary, marked by their own accents, shaped by their own histories. The Common Speech is not one language but many: a family of dialects that share enough to speak across the dark and differ enough to place a voice — its origin, its community, its experience of the void.
