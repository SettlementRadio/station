**What this covers** — the material world: what's scarce and what's abundant, what moves between
worlds and what's too slow to bother, and what "money" even means when a shipment takes weeks. The
economics of light-lag underwrite everyday life.

**Building blocks to consider**
- Currency / units of value — shared across worlds, or local and exchanged?
- What's actually traded between worlds (and what stays local because distance kills it).
- Scarcity vs. post-scarcity — likely uneven, world by world.
- Labour, ownership, and how value crosses a gap measured in weeks.
- Who is rich, who is poor, and *why* — the geography of fortune.

**The tribute** — the economics-as-worldbuilding strain: abundance and its discontents, treated with
moral seriousness rather than as set-dressing.

## Canon facts

1. Because a shipment takes weeks, only the precious or the irreplaceable crosses between worlds; most of daily life is made and consumed locally.
   - **Tags:** trade, scarcity, distance, resources, local
2. Value is reckoned in a shared unit honoured across worlds, but real wealth is local and uneven — the geography of fortune follows the routes.
   - **Tags:** currency, trade, fortune, distance, scarcity
3. Abundance is real but unequal: some worlds want for nothing while others ration, and the gap is a standing moral question the station does not ignore.
   - **Tags:** abundance, scarcity, fortune, justice, inequality
