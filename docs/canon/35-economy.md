## The physics of scarcity

In the settled worlds, economics begins with mass and distance. Every gram that moves between worlds must be accelerated for the crossing, decelerated on arrival, shielded from the radiation of the between, sustained through weeks of transit. The cost of transport is not merely fuel but time, life support, the opportunity cost of cargo space that could have carried something else. This physics sets the fundamental hierarchy of value: information is cheap, mass is expensive, and the ratio between them shapes every transaction.

Air, water, and food are the base of the economic pyramid. Worlds that can produce these in surplus trade them for what they cannot make. The core, with natural gravity and free atmosphere, exports food to the stations that must grow theirs in hydroponic vats and algae tanks. The stations, in turn, export manufactured goods, refined materials, specialised knowledge. The frontier worlds, poor in both natural resources and infrastructure, import almost everything at prices that keep them permanently indebted, trading future obligations for present survival.

Movement is the blood of the economy, and movement is dear. Fuel itself is cheap — fusion runs on hydrogen, and hydrogen is everywhere, scattered through gas giants and ice and the thin dark. What is dear is the machinery that burns it: the fusion reactors and the drives, delicate things built and serviced in only a few places, dependent on rare parts and rarer skill. This trade is held by powers in their own right: the Drive Cartels of the core, the Reactor Syndicates of the industrial stations, the Independent Fitters of the frontier who keep old engines alive with borrowed equipment and desperate courage. Control of the drives is control of movement, and movement is life.

## Currency and debt across the void

The settled worlds do not share a single currency. Each world, each station, each major trading house issues notes, tokens, electronic entries that stand for value within its sphere. The Standard Credit of the core is the closest to universal, backed by the economic weight of the central worlds, accepted at most ports. But on the frontier, Standard Credits are scarce, and other currencies circulate: station scrip, corporate chits, barter obligations, the informal accounting of favours and threats that stands in for money where formal institutions are weak.

Debt is the glue that binds the economy across distance. A frontier station orders equipment from a manufacturer in the core, paying with promises that will mature when the goods arrive, when they are installed, when they generate revenue. The manufacturer extends credit because they must, because the alternative is no market, because the station's survival is in their interest. The debt grows, compounds, becomes the subject of negotiation, of restructuring, of political pressure. The frontier lives on borrowed time, borrowed money, borrowed hope.

The Exchange Houses mediate between currencies, between debts, between the present and the future. They are trusted because they must be, because trade requires it, because their collapse would be catastrophic. They charge for the service, and their fees are another tax on distance, another cost added to every transaction. The largest Exchange Houses are institutions older than many worlds, their records the closest thing to a shared memory of obligation across the settled worlds.

## The trade in essentials and luxuries

The bulk of interworld trade is in necessities: food, fuel, parts, medicine. These move in scheduled convoys, in ships that carry nothing else, in quantities reckoned to meet predictable need. The Grain Ships from the agricultural worlds, the Fuel Tankers from the refineries, the Medical Transports with their refrigerated holds of vaccines and surgical supplies. These are the arteries of the economy, and their interruption is crisis.

But trade is not only survival. Luxuries move too, in smaller quantities, at higher prices, for those who can afford them. Fresh fruit from natural gardens, carried in stasis at ruinous cost. Textiles from particular worlds, prized for their tie to place, to history, to the aesthetic of the authentic. Art, music, literature — the products of human creativity that cannot be duplicated by local effort, that carry the specific flavour of their origin. The wealthy of the core consume these as markers of status; the wealthy of the frontier consume them as connection to the wider world, as proof that they have not been abandoned.

Knowledge is the most valuable trade good and the hardest to price. Technical specifications, scientific discoveries, cultural innovations — these can be sent by radio, but their value depends on verification, on the reputation of the source, on the relationship that vouches for quality. The Knowledge Brokers of the major stations trade in information, in introductions, in the social capital that makes transactions possible. Their fees are paid in favours, in access, in the currency of influence that runs alongside the formal economy.

## The economics of the between

The ships that carry trade are themselves economic actors, weighing routes, cargoes, risks, returns. A merchant vessel is a mobile investment, its operation financed by owners who may never see it, its crew working for shares of the profit, its captain making decisions that bear on the return on capital deployed years ago. The ship must pay for its maintenance, its fuel, its crew, its financing, and still throw off surplus for the owners, for the next voyage, for the reserves against disaster.

Salvage is the dark twin of trade, the recovery of value from failure. Abandoned ships, derelict stations, the debris of collision and catastrophe — these are the raw material of the salvage economy. Salvage operators work on speculation, investing in equipment and expertise, hoping for the find that will repay the risk. The Law of Salvage governs their claims, but enforcement is local, variable, subject to the power of those who arrive first with the means to hold what they have found.

Piracy is the shadow economy, the taking of value without exchange. Raiders seize cargo, ships, sometimes crew for ransom or labour. Their economics are brutal: the cost of a fast ship, the risk of death or capture, the need for markets where stolen goods can be sold. Piracy pays for few who attempt it; the survivors are those who have learned to calculate risk, to choose their targets, to vanish before any response can arrive. The worlds suppress piracy not because it is immoral but because it threatens the trust on which trade depends, the predictability that makes investment possible.

## Core and frontier: the geometry of power

The economic map of the settled worlds is a hierarchy of dependence. The core produces food, fuel, manufactured goods, knowledge. It controls the financial institutions, the Exchange Houses, the major shipping lines. It sets the terms of trade, the standards of quality, the currencies in which debts are denominated. Its prosperity is the prosperity of the system, and its crises become everyone's crises.

The frontier is the periphery — dependent, indebted, resentful. Frontier worlds import necessities at prices set by distant markets, export raw materials at prices squeezed by distant buyers, and accumulate debt that binds them to the core. Their development is financed by core capital, on terms that ensure the profits flow outward. Their bids for independence — economic, political, military — are constrained by this dependence, by the need for imports only the core can supply.

The stations occupy the middle ground, more developed than the frontier, less independent than the core. They are the nodes of trade, the refineries of fuel, the makers of specialised goods. They are also where core and frontier meet, where the tensions of the system are most visible. Station populations are mixed: core administrators, frontier labourers, the Betweeners who belong nowhere and make everything run. The stations are where the economic order is maintained, and where it is most open to challenge.

The Children of the Signal and other marginal groups live outside this economy, or alongside it, scavenging what they can, trading in goods and services the formal economy excludes. They are the reminder that the order is not total — that there are ways of surviving that do not depend on the Exchange Houses and the Standard Credit, that the settled worlds hold possibilities the core has not captured.
