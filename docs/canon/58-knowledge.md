## Schools and the Common Lessons

Every settled child is schooled, and no two worlds school alike — except for the core they share. The Common Lessons are a relay-distributed curriculum kept in slow consensus across the settled worlds: the shared clock and how to read it, the distances learned by heart, the Compacts every settled person is taught to honour, the history of the crossings told plainly, and the practical competences — air, water, repair — that the frontier ethos holds no one should lack. Each world wraps the Lessons in its own language, faith, and local knowledge, so two children a month apart by ship learn the same spine of things inside very different schools. Children are schooled communally, in the household way; the teacher is a respected trade everywhere, and on small stations the teacher is usually also something else — medic, hydroponicist, archivist — because no skill travels alone out there.

## Correspondence science

Research in the settled worlds is a conversation conducted at the speed of the queue. A question posed under one sun is answered under another, seasons later; a career is measured in exchanges, not papers, and the great collaborations look like the Chorus of Nine — minds that never meet, building across the lag. The relays carry results cheaply, but carrying is not knowing: the settled worlds' rule of verification is that **a result is not real until a stranger has repeated it under another sun**. Replication across worlds is the highest scientific honour and the slowest, and the patience this demands has shaped the science itself — the worlds are strong in the long observational disciplines, in astronomy and materials and ecology and the statistics of small populations, and weak in anything that needs a hundred specialists in one room.

Because the lag makes priority disputes both inevitable and unresolvable, custom has disarmed them: when two worlds reach the same result independently — and they constantly do — it is recorded as a **twin discovery**, both names attached, neither first. Arguing over precedence across weeks of lag is regarded as core vanity; the twin registry is one of the Settlement Council's quiet successes, and some researchers deliberately seek out a "twin" on a far world to check their work before they trust it.

## Academies, practical schools, and the Circuit Scholars

The core keeps the old academies — institutions older than some worlds, with archives, instruments, and the luxury of pure research; a frontier family that sends a child to a core academy is making a decade-long bet, and the child who returns (many do not return) comes back half a stranger. The frontier keeps practical schools instead, where research is indistinguishable from survival: better scrubbers, hardier crops, medicine that works in a two-room clinic. Neither tradition quite respects the other, and both are right about what the other is missing.

Between them travel the Circuit Scholars — teachers and researchers who ride the ships, teaching in transit and in port, carrying the newest work outward and the frontier's hard-won practical knowledge back in. A scholar's circuit takes years; crews prize them as passengers (a crossing with a scholar aboard is a crossing with a school in it), and a station that hosts one for a season treats it as a windfall. Medicine trains this way too: by apprenticeship, and by the relay casebooks — annotated case records that circulate on the queue, argued over and appended world by world, the profession's slow shared memory.

## What the worlds refuse to study

Some silences are chosen. No one researches faster-than-light, because the universe has spoken and the answer is no (`75-technology.md`) — proposing it is how a researcher stops being taken seriously. The engineering of minds is restricted, monitored, sometimes banned, pursued only at the edges (`25-other-minds.md`). And the Search for Others goes on precisely because it is *not* refused: patient, funded, centuries old, finding only more silence and counting that as knowledge too. The settled worlds are not incurious; they are careful about which questions can be safely loved.
