## The heavy-worlders

The heavy-worlders are those who live where gravity pulls harder than Earth ever did. Their bodies are shorter, denser, their bones thicker, their hearts more powerful. They move slowly, deliberately, conserving energy in an environment that punishes waste. On standard-gravity worlds they are uncomfortable, their joints aching, their balance uncertain, their bodies built for a pull that no longer holds them. They are a reminder that human form is not fixed, that the body answers its conditions, that what is "normal" is only ever local.

The heavy-worlders have grown cultures that prize endurance and deliberation. Their art runs to the monumental, their music to the slow and deep, their dealings to the formal and sustained. Others see them as dour, patient, reliable — sometimes as plodding, unimaginative, stuck. They see themselves as grounded, literal, rooted in a physical reality their lighter cousins can afford to ignore. The heavy-worlds are generally poor, dependent on trade for what they cannot make, their exports mostly raw materials and the labour of their strong bodies. Their resentment of the lighter worlds is old and deep.

## The light-worlders

The light-worlders live where gravity is gentler than Earth-standard — on small planets, moons, orbital habitats where the body moves freely, where the effort of living is less. Their bodies are taller, thinner, their bones more fragile, their muscles less developed. They move quickly, gracefully, their gestures wide in the space that light gravity allows. On standard or heavy worlds they are weak, their hearts labouring, their bones at risk under loads they were never built to bear.

The light-worlders have grown cultures that prize speed and reach. Their art runs to the aerial, the mobile, the ephemeral; their music is fast, their dealings quick and intense, their attention moving rapidly from one thing to the next. Others see them as flighty, superficial, impatient — sometimes as free, creative, unburdened. They see themselves as liberated, their bodies released from the tyranny of weight that binds their heavier cousins. The light-worlds are often wealthy, their orbital stations centres of manufacturing and research, their populations educated and mobile. Their disdain for the heavy-worlders is casual and pervasive.

## The Betweeners

The Betweeners are those who live in the ships and stations, in the transit corridors and the relay outposts, never settling on any world. They are born in zero gravity, or in the spun gravity of rotation, their bodies shaped to conditions that planet-dwellers find alien. They are taller than heavy-worlders, more compact than light-worlders, their bodies answering the particular demands of their environments: the need to move in three dimensions, to conserve resources, to endure the radiation and isolation of the void.

The Betweeners have grown a culture that prizes adaptability and self-sufficiency. They are the traders, the pilots, the engineers, the go-betweens of the worlds. They speak a dialect that blends the tongues of a dozen planets, they keep customs that are portable, they form relationships intense and temporary, suited to the brief encounters of transit. Planet-dwellers see them as rootless, mercenary, untrustworthy — sometimes as cosmopolitan, sophisticated, free. They see themselves as the truest humans of the settled worlds, the ones who have adapted most fully to existence between the stars, who have made a home of the void itself. It is among the Betweeners that the journey-is-destination conviction runs deepest — held as outright faith only by the small Wayfarer creed, but present as a temperament in many who live in transit: the sense that the crossing, not the arrival, is the point.

The Betweeners include the Children of the Signal, those who have chosen to live in the relay stations, keeping the network that binds the worlds together. They are a subculture within the Betweeners — more isolated, more dedicated, stranger. They speak of the relays as living things, they grow rituals around the maintenance of equipment, they form communities that span the distance between stations through their shared labour and their shared listening.

## The world-bound and the world-lost

Most humans still live on worlds — on planets with natural gravity and atmosphere, however modified. They are the world-bound, those for whom the planetary surface is still the default, the natural, the proper place for human life. They are as various as the worlds themselves: the people of desert planets, of ocean worlds, of forest moons, of volcanic rock barely habitable. Each world has shaped its people, and each people has shaped its world — the slow loop of adaptation and terraforming producing kinds of human experience the first settlers could never have foreseen.

The world-lost are those who have left their natal worlds and cannot go back. The heavy-worlder who moves to a standard world finds their body failing; the light-worlder who settles on a planet finds themselves trapped by gravity; the Betweener who tries to settle finds themselves restless, unable to sleep in a bed that does not move, unable to bear the open sky. The world-lost are a growing population, the casualties of the very mobility the settled worlds permit. They gather in the stations, in the orbital habitats, in the places that come closest to the conditions they have adapted to. They are a reminder that adaptation does not reverse, that the human form is plastic but not endlessly so, that the price of variety is sometimes exile from a whole range of human experience.

## Kinship, gender & the shapes of belonging

Bodies are not the only thing the worlds reshaped. Kinship and gender vary as widely as gravity does. Some worlds keep the old forms — the couple, the bloodline, the family name carried down. Others count kin by household rather than blood, or by ship and crew, so that a Betweener introducing their "mother" may mean the navigator who raised them between ports. Several worlds have grown social genders the old words never anticipated — roles named for work, for season, for the stage of a life — and a traveller learns to ask rather than assume. None of this is treated as exotic in the settled worlds; it is treated like dialect. Each world's arrangements are locally obvious, mutually strange, and translatable with patience, and the station's DJs — who speak to all of them at once — long ago learned the grammar of asking.

## The question of the human

The settled worlds have not resolved the question of what human means. The word is used, needed, heavy with history and hope. But it strains at the edges. Are the heavy-worlders and the light-worlders the same kind? They can have children together, but those children are often frail, caught between adaptations that do not combine well. Are the Betweeners human in the same way as the world-bound, with cultures that planet-dwellers can barely follow, bodies shaped to conditions that would kill their ancestors? Are the Children of the Signal still human, with their rituals and their isolation, their bodies marked by the radiation of the relays?

The legal answer is that all are human, owed the rights and protections of the Compacts. The biological answer is that the settled worlds hold a spread of variation that earlier ages would have called the beginning of speciation. The cultural answer is that humanity is a choice, a commitment, a way of being that is learned and kept rather than simply given. The practical answer is that the worlds must deal with one another — must trade, must speak, must find ways to recognise kinship across differences that once would have seemed absolute.

The prejudices of the settled worlds follow these lines of difference. The heavy-worlders look down on the light-worlders as weak, decadent, overreaching. The light-worlders look down on the heavy-worlders as crude, limited, stuck in the mud. The planet-dwellers look down on the Betweeners as rootless, amoral, not fully human. The Betweeners look down on the planet-dwellers as provincial, bound by gravity and air, unable to see the larger picture. These prejudices are not universal — there are individuals who rise above them, communities that mix, places where the lines blur — but they are stubborn, shaping the meetings of peoples, the politics of the Settlement Council, the patterns of migration and settlement.
