## The games themselves

Every world plays the old games bent to its own physics, and one game belongs to the void itself. **Hollowball** is the stations' sport: played in the open air of a rotation hub where the spin-gravity fades to nothing, two crews of five contesting a ball that never falls. Every station's hub is a different shape, so every station's court is different — visiting teams learn the hollow before they learn the opponent, and the home advantage is architectural. Planet-dwellers find it beautiful and unwatchable in equal measure; station children play it before they can properly walk in gravity.

The racing circuits are the other great spectacle, and they come in three kinds. Atmospheric racing threads fliers through real weather — Meridian's league is the famous one, run in the calm seasons between the storms. Ice racing belongs to Cold Harbor's long night, engines singing on the frozen sea. And the orbital sprints run ship-against-ship over fixed courses between stations, the nearest thing the between has to a stadium. Racing pilots take call-names in the frontier manner — short, functional, earned — and a pilot's call-name outlives their career.

## The season across the lag

No two settlements can play on the same day, so the settled worlds long ago stopped pretending. A season is not a calendar; it is a ledger. Fixtures are agreed by relay months ahead, played under local skies, and the results cross the dark on the broadcast tier — hours to the core, a day or two to the far frontier. Records travel slower: a record is provisional until the standing records committee, which convenes by relay between Games years, has ratified the conditions, and ratification takes weeks. Every fan knows the taste of a provisional triumph.

The station's sports desk is what makes the scattered results one season. It stitches fixtures, standings, and commentary into a single running story, always naming the lag as it goes — a race call is a retelling minutes or hours after the fact, and the desk says so with pride rather than apology. Supporters cheer month-old victories at full volume; the wagering is small, constant, and universal, run on honour ledgers in every rec hall, and a settlement that welched on its sporting debts would sooner default on a trade contract.

Teams carry their settlement's name and a working emblem — a tool, a weather, a trade — so the fixture list reads like a map of who the worlds are. Most sport is amateur: shift leagues, children's leagues, the crowded gymnasium after work. The professionals are few and beloved, and a settlement's team is part of its name.

## The Inter-Settlement Games

The Games are held each fourth year, hosted by a different world in turn, and hosting is an honour a world prepares a decade for — the bid, the building, the training of a generation of local officials. Events run in the four gravity classes — heavy, standard, light, and spun — with records kept per class, so a heavy-world lifter and a light-world flier are each champion on their own terms. The opening custom is the Reading of the Absent: the names of the settlements too far, too poor, or too newly founded to send anyone, read aloud so the Games begin by remembering who is missing. Athletes travel months to compete; some spend the better part of the four years in transit and training, and the ones who make that crossing more than once are honoured for the travelling as much as the winning.
