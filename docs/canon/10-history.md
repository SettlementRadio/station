## The long leaving

Humanity left Earth not in a single exodus but in a long, uneven diaspora that stretched across centuries. The first ships were slow — generation vessels carrying thousands who would live and die and bear children in the dark, their arrival at distant stars uncertain and lifetimes away. The leaving was never organised, never centrally planned; it was the accumulation of millions of separate decisions — desperate flights, sponsored colonisations, commercial ventures, migrations of faith, scientific expeditions that became permanent homes. It is remembered now less as one event than as a slow tide outward.

Earth is remembered as distant origin, spoken of fondly in the classrooms of a thousand worlds, visited rarely by the few who can afford the journey and the years it takes. What every settled child learns of Earth is half record, half founding myth: the blue planet, the cradle, the place where humanity first learned to look up and wonder. The reality is less romantic. Earth is one polity among many now, no longer dominant, its resources worn by ages of use, its population a fraction of what it was at the diaspora's height. The fondness is real, but it is the fondness of the child for the parent they have outgrown, the gratitude of those who made their own way.

The diaspora made the settled worlds not as a single civilisation but as a scattering of seeds, each growing by its own soil and light. The early colonies were isolated for generations, their only contact with home the rare ship that might arrive long after it set out, carrying news already ancient. They grew their own cultures, their own tongues, their own ways of ordering life to fit their particular conditions. The unity of humanity was not preserved; it was re-made, slowly, through the hard work of establishing contact, trade, and the relays that at last made speech across the dark possible.

## The ages of expansion and loss

The history of the settled worlds runs in long swings of expansion and contraction. The First Expansion saw the founding of the core, its first stable colonies, the slow building of the relay network and the sublight shipping that knit the new region together — until worlds that had been generations apart by sail lay only weeks apart by ship and mere hours apart by signal. It was an age of optimism, of faith in progress, of the belief that humanity's troubles could be outrun by distance and new beginnings. Its records are fragmentary, kept in the archives of the oldest worlds, but its spirit is still felt in the architecture of the core cities and the institutions that trace their line to those early centuries.

The Silence followed — the long age when contact between worlds broke down. The relay network decayed faster than it could be maintained; the knowledge of building and tending it was lost on worlds that had come to depend on it. Worlds that had traded for centuries fell out of reach; some were lost entirely, their locations forgotten, their people presumed dead until a chance rediscovery generations later. The Silence was not uniform — some regions held contact, some knowledge was preserved, some worlds even thrived alone while others failed — but the overall pattern was contraction, the dark growing wide again, each settlement coming to believe itself alone. It is the Silence the songs and stories still reach back to, the age whose desperation bred the worst of the old wars.

The Reconnection came slowly, as the relays were rebuilt, as old routes were replotted, as the worlds found one another again. It was an age of recovery as much as exploration — of relearning what had been lost, of re-establishing ties that had lapsed for centuries. The Reconnection was no simple return to the First Expansion; the worlds that met again were changed, their tongues diverged, their customs strange to one another. But it restored the possibility of unity, of the shared civilisation the Silence had broken.

## The current age and its uncertainties

The present age — the Age of the Relays — began with the building of the first stable relay network in centuries, the return of regular speech between the worlds, the founding of the Settlement Council and the common institutions that bind the scattered human presence into something like one civilisation. It is an age of consolidation: of working out the relationships thrown together in haste during the Reconnection, of growing a shared culture through the medium of the relays.

But the age is not stable. The frontier keeps pushing outward, new worlds founded, new settlements raised in the dark beyond the mapped routes. The core grows wealthy and powerful, its populations dense, its institutions elaborate, its grip on the Settlement Council tightening. The stations multiply, the Betweeners increase, the people of the void become an ever-larger share of humanity. The settled worlds are changing, and the direction of the change is far from clear.

There are rumours of new arrangements that might ease the frontier's isolation — better ships, denser relays — though none that could ever defeat distance itself. There are fears of another Silence, of the relays failing, of the fragile threads that bind the worlds proving as temporary as those of earlier ages. There are hopes of contact with others, of the silence of the dark finally ending, of humanity learning that it is not alone. These rumours and fears and hopes are part of the present, shaping the choices of governments and individuals — the investments and migrations and conflicts that make up the ongoing history of the settled worlds.

## The work of memory

History in the settled worlds is work, not merely record. The distances that separate the worlds mean that archives are scattered, that records are lost, that the past must be rebuilt from fragments and inference. Each world keeps its own history, its own memory of the diaspora and the Silence and the Reconnection, and these memories do not always agree. The Settlement Council has sponsored efforts at a common chronology, but the work is slow and contested.

The founding myths of the worlds often quarrel with the records. A world may claim descent from a particular ship, a particular expedition, a particular hero of the diaspora, and the archival evidence may bear this out or cut against it. The disagreements are not merely academic; they touch claims to territory, to resources, to precedence in the Settlement Council. The work of the historian is thus political, sometimes dangerous, exposed to pressure from those whose interests a particular version of the past would serve.

The forgotten are those worlds, settlements, and expeditions that have left no trace, or whose traces have not yet been found. The settled worlds know their history is incomplete — that there are gaps that may never be filled, that there may be worlds still inhabited, still growing, still waiting to be found again. The search for these lost worlds is an ongoing project, funded by governments and private patrons, carried out by explorers who join the skills of the archaeologist to those of the navigator. The discovery of a lost world is an event of the first magnitude — a reshaping of the understood past, a reminder that the history of the settled worlds is still being written.
