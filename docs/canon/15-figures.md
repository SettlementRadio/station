# 15-figures.md — legends & remembered names

> **Cornerstone file — the dead and the legendary: the names the worlds actually reach for.**
> **Ownership rule (hard):** the bible names ONLY the dead and the legendary. Living, present-day
> named people are **dynamic world state** — the tick's `figures`/`quotes` tables (D10) — and are
> never hand-authored here; hand-authoring a living figure would create two competing sources of
> who's-who. One standing exception stays canon: the station's own founders left no names
> (`00-station.md` fact 7) — never give them any.
>
> Every entry is *legend-framed* on purpose: history in the settled worlds is contested and
> half-mythical (`10-history.md`), so a DJ may retell, embroider, or doubt these stories freely —
> what is pinned is the name, the deed as remembered, and what the worlds use the memory *for*.
> See `docs/canon/README.md` for the authoring conventions.

## Legends of the crossings

**Marah Onduile, the Wayfinder.** The diaspora-era navigator credited with charting the first stable lanes between the scattered worlds — credited, that is, by everyone at once: half a dozen worlds claim her landfall, two claim her birth, and the archival record supports almost none of it. That is the point of her. When a captain finds a clean route through bad space they say she *onduiled* it; when two worlds quarrel over precedence they both invoke her, and the quarrel becomes a joke that cools the room. She is remembered less as a person than as the settled worlds' shared claim to having found their own way.

**Brekka Voss, the Rediscoverer.** The Reconnection-era explorer who found the first of the lost worlds — a settlement the Silence had swallowed generations before, still inhabited, still waiting. Her logs, taught in every navigation school, founded the craft that joins the archaeologist to the navigator; her sign-off line — *"They were never lost. We were."* — is quoted whenever a search is argued for. The rediscovered world itself keeps its own, very different version of the story, in which she arrived late, badly provisioned, and was rescued by the people she is famous for finding. Both versions are told on air.

## Legends of the Silence

**The Quiet Engineer.** The name is lost; only the deed is kept. Somewhere in the worst of the Silence, one keeper stayed with a failing relay and kept it alive alone — patching, rationing, transmitting the maintenance beacon on schedule — for some forty years. When a ship finally reached the node, the relay was working and the keeper was long dead, logs in order, tools cleaned and racked. The Children of the Signal keep the Quiet Engineer as their patron-legend, and relay technicians across the worlds still rack their tools at shift's end "for the Quiet one." Every region of the settled worlds is certain the relay was theirs.

**Halden Mere, the Mercy Round.** A Silence-era physician who rode a circuit of cut-off worlds by ship, carrying medicine, technique, and news of who still lived — decades of crossings, most of them one-way risks. Frontier medics still call a tour of remote settlements *riding the Mercy Round*, and the phrase is the profession's highest compliment. The records disagree about almost everything else: whether Mere was one person or a title passed between physicians, and the medics like it that way — it means anyone can pick the Round up.

## Names given to agreements

**Teller Ashe, the Clock-Keeper.** Settlement Time was agreed by committees over a generation (`01-time.md`), but history gives the agreement her face: the Reconnection-era archivist remembered for reconciling the diverged calendars world by world, ledger by ledger, until one shared count was possible. The records show a hundred hands; the story keeps one name, because a civilisation cannot toast a committee. On New Year's, some worlds still raise the first glass "to Teller."

**Sef Varan-Idri, the Mediator.** The negotiator remembered for ending the War of the Three Stations with the Treaty of Concordance (`45-conflict.md`), and for the sentence at its heart: *"Possession is provisional."* Accounts of the mediation differ in every telling — how long it took, who broke first, whether the famous sentence was diplomacy or exhaustion — but mediators are still trained on the transcripts, and calling someone *a Varan-Idri* means they can sit in a room where everyone is wrong and leave it with a signature.

**Mother Ledger.** The ship-born arbiter of the early transit age whose rulings, passed captain to captain, hardened into the customs now called the Law of Salvage (`40-law.md`). No portrait, no home port, no registry — just the rulings, each one a story with a judgement at the end, still recited in the between the way other traditions recite scripture: *the ship that answers a distress call owns nothing but its honour, and owes nothing but its speed.*

## Legends of the arts

**Odessa Vail.** The composer of a single towering work — *Lanternlight*, the Core Harmony cycle written for the first Lumen Festival she lived to see — and, so far as the record shows, of almost nothing else. The mystery of the silence after is argued in every music programme: perfection reached, or nerve lost, or (the romantic version) a second cycle sent by relay toward a lover on the frontier and never received. When a work is finished and its maker stops, the worlds say the artist *went to the lanterns*.

**Corin Hale, the Vigilkeeper.** A lifetime spent on one lonely relay outpost, emerging in old age with the *Station Cycles* — the void ballads that redefined what isolation could express (`70-music.md`), built around the actual drone of the outpost's life support, which Hale refused to have repaired because it had become the tonic note. Night-shift DJs across the settled worlds keep a Hale piece within reach the way other people keep a lamp.

**The Chorus of Nine.** The collective whose members never met — nine composers on nine worlds, collaborating entirely by relay across the dark, each movement crossing the lag to be answered a season later. Their names are known; the *order* of their contributions is not, and they refused to clarify it while they lived, saying the piece belonged to the distance. They are the standing proof that the lag can be a collaborator instead of an enemy.

**The Anonymous of the Latecomers.** The most famous name the settled worlds do not have. The author of the most widely read novel in existence (`65-arts.md`) published it namelessly from a frontier station generations ago, and every attempt at attribution since has failed or been refuted. Guessing the Anonymous is a parlour game, an academic sub-field, and a running joke on the station's book programmes; canon's position is the culture's position — **the anonymity is part of the work, and it holds.**
